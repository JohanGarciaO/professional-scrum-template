# Product Goal

Define the primary objective that guides the development of your product.

---

## About this document

This document serves both as a learning guide and as a project template.

If this is your first time defining a Product Goal, read the explanatory sections before filling out the template.

Once you understand how to use this document, feel free to remove the explanatory sections and keep only the completed template for your project.

---

## Purpose

The Product Goal defines the single long-term objective that the Scrum Team is working toward.

Unlike the Product Vision, which explains why the product exists, the Product Goal describes what the team is currently trying to achieve.

It provides direction for Product Backlog refinement, Sprint Planning, and prioritization decisions.

A Product Goal should remain stable until it is achieved or intentionally replaced by a new goal.

---

## Who is responsible?

The Product Goal is created and maintained by the **Product Owner**.

Although the Product Owner is responsible for defining it, the Scrum Team should understand and support the goal, as it guides the development of the product.

---

## When should it be created?

The Product Goal should be defined after establishing the Product Vision and before creating or refining the Product Backlog.

Whenever backlog prioritization becomes uncertain, the Product Goal should be used as the primary decision-making reference.

A new Product Goal should only be defined after the current one has been achieved or intentionally abandoned.

---

## How to write a good Product Goal

A good Product Goal should answer the following questions:

- What is the primary objective the team is trying to achieve?
- What outcome defines success?
- How does this objective support the Product Vision?

Keep the Product Goal focused on a single objective.

Avoid describing implementation details, technical solutions, or multiple unrelated objectives.

The Product Goal should provide direction without prescribing how the solution will be implemented.

---

## Template

### Goal Statement

> Describe the primary objective the Scrum Team is working toward.

### Expected Outcome

> Describe the measurable outcome that indicates the goal has been achieved.

### Success Criteria

> Define the conditions that determine whether the Product Goal has been successfully accomplished.

---

## Example

### Goal Statement

Deliver a centralized platform that allows travel agencies to efficiently manage airline reservations from multiple airlines in a single place.

### Expected Outcome

Travel agencies can create, manage, and monitor airline reservations through a unified platform, reducing manual work and improving operational efficiency.

### Success Criteria

- Reservation management is fully operational.
- The platform supports the primary airline integrations.
- Travel agencies can perform their daily workflow without relying on external systems.
- The solution is stable enough to support pilot customers.

---

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ Confusing the Product Goal with the Product Vision

Bad:

> Become the leading travel management platform.

Good:

> Deliver a centralized reservation management platform for travel agencies.

---

### ❌ Defining multiple goals

Bad:

> Build authentication, dashboards, reports, notifications, and PDF generation.

Good:

> Deliver a centralized reservation management platform.

---

### ❌ Focusing on implementation

Bad:

> Implement authentication using OAuth and PostgreSQL.

Good:

> Enable agencies to securely access and manage reservations through a centralized platform.

> 💡 **Engineering Tip**
>
> If your team cannot explain the Product Goal in a single sentence, it is probably trying to accomplish too many things at once.
>
> A clear Product Goal improves prioritization and helps every Sprint contribute toward the same objective.
