# Definition of Ready (DoR)

Define the minimum conditions that a Product Backlog Item must satisfy before being selected for a Sprint.

---

## About this document

This document serves both as a learning guide and as a project template.

If this is your first time creating a Definition of Ready (DoR), read the explanatory sections before filling out the template.

Once you understand how to use it, feel free to remove the explanatory sections and keep only the completed Definition of Ready for your project.

---

## Purpose

The Definition of Ready (DoR) establishes the minimum criteria that a Product Backlog Item (PBI) must satisfy before the Scrum Team commits to implementing it during a Sprint.

Its purpose is to reduce uncertainty during Sprint Planning and ensure that the team starts development with enough information to work efficiently.

A well-defined DoR improves Sprint predictability by preventing incomplete, ambiguous, or poorly understood work from entering the Sprint Backlog.

Unlike the Definition of Done (DoD), which determines when work is finished, the Definition of Ready determines when work is ready to begin.

---

## Who is responsible?

The Definition of Ready is created collaboratively by the Scrum Team.

Although the Product Owner is responsible for maintaining the Product Backlog, every team member should participate in defining and improving the DoR, ensuring that work entering a Sprint is clear and actionable.

---

## When should it be created?

The Definition of Ready should be established at the beginning of the project and reviewed whenever the team's development process evolves.

It is primarily used during Product Backlog Refinement and Sprint Planning to determine whether a Product Backlog Item is ready for implementation.

---

## How to define a good Definition of Ready

The Definition of Ready should contain objective and verifiable criteria.

Every Product Backlog Item selected for a Sprint should satisfy all defined conditions.

Avoid creating an excessively restrictive checklist that slows down refinement activities.

Instead, focus on ensuring that the team has sufficient information to confidently begin development.

Typical readiness criteria include:

- The objective is clearly described.
- Acceptance Criteria have been defined.
- Dependencies have been identified.
- The item has been estimated.
- Questions and ambiguities have been resolved.

---

## Template

A Product Backlog Item is considered **Ready** when:

- [ ] The business objective is clearly described.
- [ ] Acceptance Criteria have been defined.
- [ ] Business rules are documented.
- [ ] Dependencies have been identified.
- [ ] External blockers have been resolved.
- [ ] The item has been estimated.
- [ ] The team understands the expected outcome.
- [ ] The Product Owner agrees that the item is ready for development.

---

## Example

### User Story

> As a travel agent, I want to authenticate using my Google account so that I can securely access the platform.

### Ready Checklist

- ☑ Business objective clearly defined.
- ☑ Acceptance Criteria documented.
- ☑ OAuth provider identified.
- ☑ UI prototype available.
- ☑ Database impact analyzed.
- ☑ Dependencies identified.
- ☑ Story estimated at 5 Story Points.
- ☑ Team has no outstanding questions.

Result:

✅ This Product Backlog Item is ready to be selected during Sprint Planning.

## Common Mistakes

### ❌ Using the DoR as an approval process

Bad:

> The Scrum Master must approve every item before development.

Good:

> The Scrum Team verifies whether the agreed readiness criteria have been satisfied.

---

### ❌ Creating an excessively long checklist

Bad:

> A checklist with dozens of mandatory items for every task.

Good:

> A concise checklist containing only the information necessary for the team to begin work confidently.

---

### ❌ Confusing Ready with Done

Bad:

> Code reviewed.

> Tests passing.

> Deployment completed.

Good:

> These belong to the **Definition of Done**, not the Definition of Ready.
> The DoR verifies whether work is prepared to start—not whether it has been completed.
